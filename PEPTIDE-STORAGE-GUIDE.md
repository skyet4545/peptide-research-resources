# Peptide Storage Guide — Temperature, Stability & Best Practices

Proper storage is critical to maintaining peptide integrity for research purposes. Degradation can render peptides ineffective and compromise research results.

## Storage Temperature Guidelines

| State | Temperature | Duration |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Lyophilized (powder, sealed) | -20°C to -80°C | 24+ months |
| Lyophilized (powder, opened) | -20°C | 6–12 months |
| Reconstituted (in bacteriostatic water) | 2–8°C (refrigerator) | 28–30 days |
| Reconstituted (in sterile water) | 2–8°C | 7 days max |
| Room temperature | Avoid | Hours only |

## Common Peptides and Special Storage Notes

### BPC-157
- Relatively stable; lyophilized form is forgiving at -20°C
- Reconstituted: use within 30 days at 2–8°C
- More info: https://peptideradar.net/blog/bpc-157-complete-guide.html

### TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4)
- Extremely stable lyophilized; one of the more resilient peptides
- Reconstituted with bacteriostatic water: stable 4–6 weeks refrigerated

### PT-141 (Bremelanotide)
- Light-sensitive: store in amber vials away from UV
- Reconstituted: use within 2 weeks

### Semaglutide / GLP-1 Analogs
- Require cold chain throughout; never freeze reconstituted form
- Store multi-dose pens at 2–8°C after first use

### GHK-Cu
- Copper peptide — avoid metal contamination
- Store away from light; refrigerate once reconstituted

## Reconstitution Best Practices

1. **Always use bacteriostatic water** (not sterile water) for multi-dose vials
2. Add water slowly down the side of the vial — never inject directly onto the lyophilized cake
3. Swirl gently — do NOT shake (causes aggregation)
4. Label every vial: date reconstituted, concentration, peptide name
5. Use 1mL insulin syringes (U-100) for accurate micro-dosing

## Freeze-Thaw Cycles
- Minimize freeze-thaw cycles — each cycle degrades peptide bonds
- Best practice: aliquot into single-use amounts before freezing

## Red Flags (Quality Indicators)
- Cloudiness after reconstitution (possible contamination or aggregation)
- Color change (yellow/brown = oxidation)
- Precipitate that won't dissolve
